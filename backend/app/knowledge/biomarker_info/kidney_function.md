# Kidney Function Test (KFT / RFT) Biomarkers

## Overview
The Kidney Function Test checks how well your kidneys are filtering waste from your blood. Your kidneys filter about 180 litres of blood daily. They also regulate blood pressure, balance electrolytes, and produce hormones for red blood cell production.

---

## Serum Creatinine

### What it measures
Creatinine is a waste product from normal muscle metabolism, constantly filtered by the kidneys. If kidneys are struggling, creatinine builds up in the blood.

### Normal Range
- Adult Male: 0.7 - 1.2 mg/dL
- Adult Female: 0.5 - 1.0 mg/dL

### HIGH Creatinine means
The kidneys are not filtering waste efficiently. This is called **azotemia**.
- **Common causes:** Dehydration (most common), high protein diet, intense exercise, diabetes, high blood pressure (leading causes of chronic kidney disease in India), kidney infections.
- **Mild elevation:** Could be temporary (dehydration). Repeat test after good hydration.
- **Consistently high:** Needs full kidney workup.

### LOW Creatinine
Often seen in low muscle mass, malnutrition, or liver disease.

### Lifestyle Tips if HIGH:
- **Hydrate:** Drink 2.5 - 3 litres of water daily.
- **Reduce protein:** Avoid excessive red meat and high-protein supplements/powders.
- **Control blood sugar and blood pressure:** These are the two biggest drivers of kidney damage in India.
- **Avoid NSAIDs regularly:** Medicines like ibuprofen (Brufen) can damage kidneys.

## BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen) / Serum Urea

### What it measures
Urea is another waste product from protein breakdown, filtered by the kidneys. Similar indicator to creatinine.

### Normal Range
- BUN: 7 - 20 mg/dL
- Serum Urea: 15 - 45 mg/dL

### HIGH BUN/Urea means
Dehydration, high protein diet, kidney disease, or bleeding in the digestive tract.

### BUN/Creatinine Ratio
Doctors use this ratio to understand the cause:
- Ratio > 20: Dehydration or a problem *before* the kidney (like internal bleeding)
- Ratio < 10: A problem *within* the kidney itself
- Ratio 10-20: Normal or mixed cause

## eGFR (Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate)

### What it measures
This is THE most important single marker of kidney function. It calculates how much blood (in mL) the kidneys filter per minute, estimated from creatinine, age, and gender.

### Normal Range (Stages of CKD)
| eGFR (mL/min) | Stage | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| ≥ 90 | Normal / Stage 1 | Normal kidney function |
| 60 - 89 | Stage 2 | Mildly reduced |
| 45 - 59 | Stage 3a | Mildly–Moderately reduced |
| 30 - 44 | Stage 3b | Moderately–Severely reduced |
| 15 - 29 | Stage 4 | Severely reduced |
| < 15 | Stage 5 | Kidney failure (dialysis consideration) |

### LOW eGFR means
Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD). Note that eGFR naturally declines with age, especially after 60+.

## Uric Acid

### What it measures
A waste product from breaking down purines (found in red meat, organ meats, seafood, alcohol, and some vegetables like spinach). Kidneys excrete it. Build-up causes gout.

### Normal Range
- Male: 3.5 - 7.2 mg/dL
- Female: 2.6 - 6.0 mg/dL

### HIGH Uric Acid (Hyperuricemia) means
- **Gout:** Crystals deposit in joints causing severe pain (commonly the big toe, knees, ankles).
- **Kidney stones:** Uric acid type.
- **Causes:** High purine diet (red meat, organ meats, beer, shrimp), dehydration, certain medications (diuretics), metabolic syndrome.

### Lifestyle Tips if HIGH:
- **Limit:** Red meat, organ meats (brain, kidney, liver), seafood, alcohol (especially beer).
- **Increase:** Water intake, low-fat dairy (protective), vitamin C.
- **Vegetables:** While spinach and mushrooms contain purines, the evidence suggests plant-based purines raise uric acid less than animal-based ones.
- **Avoid fructose-rich foods:** Packaged juices, soft drinks with high-fructose corn syrup.

## Electrolytes: Sodium, Potassium, Chloride

### Sodium (Na)
- **Normal Range:** 136 - 145 mEq/L
- **Low (Hyponatremia):** Excessive water intake, kidney/liver/heart failure, certain medications.
- **High (Hypernatremia):** Dehydration, sodium-heavy diet, diabetes insipidus.

### Potassium (K)
- **Normal Range:** 3.5 - 5.0 mEq/L
- **Low (Hypokalemia):** Vomiting, diarrhea, diuretic medications. Can cause muscle weakness and arrhythmias.
- **High (Hyperkalemia):** Kidney disease (can't excrete potassium), certain BP meds (ACE inhibitors). Can be life-threatening for the heart.

### Lifestyle Tips for Electrolytes:
- Don't add extra salt to food. Reduce processed food which is high in sodium.
- If potassium is low: Eat bananas, cooked potatoes, coconut water, avocados.
- If potassium is high (and kidney disease): Avoid bananas, potatoes — the opposite applies.

## Questions to Ask Your Doctor

- "Is my elevated creatinine from dehydration or actual kidney damage?"
- "Do I need a kidney ultrasound?"
