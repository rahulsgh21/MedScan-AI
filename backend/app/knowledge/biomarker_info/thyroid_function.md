# Thyroid Function Test Biomarkers

## Overview
The thyroid is a butterfly-shaped gland in your neck that produces hormones controlling metabolism — how fast your body uses energy, how warm you stay, and how organs work. Thyroid disorders are among the most common hormonal conditions in India, especially in women.

---

## TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone)

### What it measures
TSH is produced by the pituitary gland in the brain. It tells the thyroid to produce more hormones. TSH is the most sensitive and first-line test for thyroid dysfunction.

### Normal Range: 0.4 - 4.0 mIU/L
(Some labs use 0.5 - 5.0 — always compare to the lab's own reference range)

### Relationship is INVERSE (Critical to understand):
- **HIGH TSH → Thyroid is UNDERactive (Hypothyroidism)** — The pituitary is "shouting" at the thyroid to make more hormones, so TSH goes up.
- **LOW TSH → Thyroid is OVERactive (Hyperthyroidism)** — The pituitary senses too much thyroid hormone and stops asking for more, so TSH goes down.

### HIGH TSH (Hypothyroidism) Symptoms
Weight gain, fatigue, cold intolerance, hair loss, dry skin, constipation, slow heart rate, brain fog, heavy periods in women.

### LOW TSH (Hyperthyroidism) Symptoms
Weight loss, rapid heart rate (palpitations), sweating, anxiety, tremors, light or missed periods, diarrhea, heat intolerance.

---

## Free T4 (Thyroxine) / FT4

### What it measures
The main hormone produced by the thyroid gland itself. "Free" T4 is the portion not bound to proteins — the active fraction.

### Normal Range: 0.8 - 1.8 ng/dL

### Interpretation with TSH:
| TSH | Free T4 | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| High | Low | Overt Hypothyroidism |
| High | Normal | Subclinical Hypothyroidism |
| Low | High | Overt Hyperthyroidism |
| Low | Normal | Subclinical Hyperthyroidism |
| Normal | Normal | Normal Thyroid Function |

---

## Free T3 (Triiodothyronine) / FT3

### What it measures
The more potent, active form of thyroid hormone. Most T3 is converted from T4 in peripheral tissues.

### Normal Range: 2.3 - 4.2 pg/mL

### Interpretation
T3 is usually tested when hyperthyroidism is suspected, or when symptoms persist despite normal TSH/T4. Low T3 can occur in severe non-thyroid illnesses.

---

## Anti-TPO Antibodies (Thyroid Peroxidase Antibody)

### What it measures
These are antibodies that attack the thyroid. Their presence indicates an **autoimmune thyroid disease**.

### Normal: < 34 IU/mL (reference ranges vary by lab)

### HIGH Anti-TPO means
- **Hashimoto's Thyroiditis:** Most common cause of hypothyroidism in India. The immune system attacks the thyroid, gradually destroying it.
- **Graves' Disease:** Autoimmune cause of hyperthyroidism.

### Note
A person can have high Anti-TPO antibodies with a normal TSH. This means the thyroid is still compensating, but there is a risk of developing hypothyroidism in the future. Follow-up testing every 6-12 months is recommended.

## Thyroid and Iodine (India-Specific Context)

### Why Iodine Matters
Iodine deficiency is a public health issue in certain parts of India (especially Himalayan and sub-Himalayan regions, and areas far from the coast). The thyroid needs iodine to make T3 and T4.
- **Iodine deficiency → Goitre (enlarged thyroid) + Hypothyroidism**
- **Using iodised salt** is the primary public health intervention.

## Lifestyle Tips for Thyroid Health

### For Hypothyroidism (High TSH):
- Take thyroid medication (Thyroxine/Eltroxin) on an empty stomach in the morning, 30-60 minutes before food.
- **Do NOT take with:** Calcium supplements, antacids, iron supplements — they reduce drug absorption.
- **Limited Goitrogens:** Cruciferous vegetables (cabbage, cauliflower, broccoli, radish) in very large raw quantities can interfere with thyroid. Cooking neutralizes this effect.
- **Selenium-rich foods:** Brazil nuts, sunflower seeds, mushrooms — selenium is needed for T4 to T3 conversion.
- **Use iodised salt.**

### For Hyperthyroidism (Low TSH):
- Avoid excess iodine (iodised salt, seaweed, supplements).
- Manage stress — stress hormones worsen hyperthyroidism.
- Eat regular, calorie-sufficient meals — hyperthyroidism burns energy faster.
- Regular cardiac monitoring if palpitations are present.
